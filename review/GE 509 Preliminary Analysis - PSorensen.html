Forests in the northeastern U.S. are projected to experience a reduction in the depth and duration of snow cover during the next century. Because snow insulates soils from freezing air, soils are expected to experience colder winter temperatures and an increased number of freeze - thaw events. Soil microbes are likely to be directly affected by the physiological stress and disruption of soil aggregates associated with soil freezing. Also, co-occurring root mortality caused by increased soil freezing could indirectly effect microbial biomass and activity due to winter climate induced changes in plant-microbe interactions. We are taking advantage of a natural winter climate gradient occurring at Hubbard Brook Experimental Forest in an attempt to isolate direct winter climate effects on microbes from winter climate change effects on microbes that arise due to co-occuring winter climate effects on plant roots.

For my dataset we have root biomass, microbial enzyme activity, and net N mineralization data from root ingrowth (2 mm mesh) or root exclusion cores (50 µm mesh) that were harvested repeatedly from 6 sites at Hubbard Brook Experimental Forest during July 2012, October 2012, and October 2013. Independent variables measured at each site are winter snow and soil frost depth and duration, soil temperature, volumetric moisture content, soil core type (ingrowth versus exclusion). Three sites are located on north-facing slopes at high elevation (535m, 555m, and 595m asl.) and three sites are located on south facing slopes at low elevation (375m, 411m, and 511m asl.). Nested within each site are three 1 m x 2 m plots (18 plots total). Nested within each plot are 3 litter-boxes (54 litter boxes total). Nested within each litter box are duplicate cores of each core type (108 ingrowth or exclusion cores across the winter climate gradient).

The goal is to estimate root biomass and production, enzyme activity, and net N mineralization from the soils collected from across the natural winter gradient. I am thinking about this dataset as a generalized mixed effects problem  the fixed effects I am interested in are core type, harvest date, and the continuous covariates measured at each site which include winter snow and soil frost depth and duration, minimum winter soil temperature, and soil temperature or volumetric moisture content at the time of soil sampling. The random effects are sites, plots, litter-boxes, and duplicate cores. The objectives I am addressing are (1) to determine whether root production differs across the gradient (2) whether microbial biomass, enzyme activity, or net N mineralization rates differ by core type or as a function of winter snow / frost depth, volumetric water content, or soil temperature at the time of sampling (3) to use model selection to determine the most parsimonious models of root production, microbial enzyme activity, and net N mineralization from across the winter climate gradient.

Objective 1 - Root Biomass Data Analysis

Root biomass was determined by root-picking all roots in ingrowth and exclusion cores on all dates (July 2012, October 2012, October 2013). The questions that I want to determine from the root biomass data set is (1) whether root biomass is greater in the ingrowth versus the exclusion cores (2) if root production is related to winter climate (3) if the difference changes by soil horizon, over sampling dates

The histogram shows that data is centered at low root biomass and skewed to the right. There were many cores that had zero root biomass. I think it is most appropriate to say the root biomass data follows an exponential data distribution.

hist(root.wcs$Root_dry_mass_g[which(root.wcs$Core_Type=="50um Exclusion")],

In a perfect world there would be no root biomass in the exclusion cores. However, some of the exclusion cores failed and ended up having roots within the core

Because the ingrowth and exclusion cores started out root-free when they were installed in the field in May 2011, I expected the ingrowth cores to become more 'rooty' at each subsequent harvest date (July 2012, October 2012, October 2013)

As such, it might be that the influence of the presence or absence of roots on microbial activity increases as the ingrowth cores get more root biomass.

One challenge of this data set is apparent from the figure above, which is that most of the covariates measured along the gradient are highly correlated. For example, snow depth and duration and minimum winter soil temperature are both positively correlated with each other…and with nitrification in the organic soil horizon(panels e and f ).

I want to do model selection in order to determine the most parsimonious model of microbial activity and deal with the correlation among soil climate covariates along the gradient.
